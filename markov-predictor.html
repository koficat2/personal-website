How it works

A Markov Chain is a probabilistic model where the next state depends only on the current state — not the full history. Applied to text, it means each word (or sequence of words) has a probability distribution over what comes next, learned entirely from the training corpus.

P(wₙ | wₙ₋₁, wₙ₋₂, ..., wₙ₋ₖ) ≈ count(wₙ₋ₖ...wₙ) / count(wₙ₋ₖ...wₙ₋₁)

This tool builds an n-th order Markov chain from your input text. Order 1 looks at one previous word; Order 4 looks at the last 4 — producing more coherent but less novel text. Temperature controls how the probability distribution is sampled: at temperature 1.0 it samples proportionally; lower temperatures concentrate probability on the most likely next word; higher temperatures flatten the distribution toward uniform randomness.

Entropy measures the average unpredictability of each state in bits: H = −Σ p(x) log₂ p(x). High entropy means many equally likely transitions (creative, varied output). Low entropy means the model is very sure what comes next (deterministic, repetitive output). Perplexity = 2^H, and represents the effective branching factor at each step.
